Conceptual Foundations

Understanding the legal framework that governs online expression in the Philippines.

Constitution

Freedom of Expression

1987 Philippine Constitution, Article III, Section 4

"No law shall be passed abridging the freedom of speech, of expression, or of the press, or the right of the people peaceably to assemble and petition the government for redress of grievances."

This right extends to digital spaces — social media posts, blogs, and online commentary are all covered.

RA 10175

Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012

Republic Act No. 10175

Signed on September 12, 2012, RA 10175 criminalizes offenses committed through information and communications technology, including hacking, identity theft, and — most controversially — cyber libel.

Cyber libel carries a penalty one degree higher than traditional libel under the Revised Penal Code.

Key Terms

Definitions

  • Libel — A public and malicious imputation of a crime, vice, or defect (real or imaginary) that tends to dishonor a person. Punishable under the Revised Penal Code.
  • Cyber Libel — Libel committed through a computer system or other similar means. Penalty: 6 years and 1 day to 12 years imprisonment.
  • Online Harassment — Repeated, unwanted contact or threatening behavior directed at a person through digital platforms. May also constitute unjust vexation or grave threats.

Why This Matters

The tension between freedom of expression and cybercrime law is one of the defining legal debates of the digital age. The Philippine Constitution was written in 1987 — before the internet, before social media, before viral posts. Yet its protections are broad enough to cover digital speech.

RA 10175, passed in 2012, was the legislature's attempt to address crimes that the old Revised Penal Code could not anticipate. But critics argue that provisions like cyber libel — which carries heavier penalties than traditional libel — have a chilling effect on free expression, particularly for journalists and ordinary citizens who speak out online.

Understanding both the constitutional guarantee and the statutory limits is essential for any Filipino navigating the digital public square.
